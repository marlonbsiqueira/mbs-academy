Module 01 · Diagnosing the Finance Function

The Finance Function Maturity Model

Why most finance functions are stuck

Ask a CFO what their finance team's job is, and they will almost certainly say something about providing accurate, timely financial information to support decision-making. Ask the same CFO how much of their team's time is actually spent on that — and the answer is usually uncomfortable.

In most organisations, the finance function is doing the opposite of what it claims: spending 60–80% of its time on data collection, reconciliation, and report production — and maybe 20% on the analysis and insight that actually matters.

This is not a failure of intelligence or effort. It is a structural problem. And it is fixable.

The four maturity levels

The Finance Function Maturity Model describes four distinct states that finance teams can occupy. Understanding which state you are in is the first step to moving forward.

Level 1 — Transactional factory

Finance exists to process transactions and produce mandated reports. The team is reactive. Leadership does not ask finance for input — they ask for numbers after the fact. Manual processes dominate. Data lives in spreadsheets. Month-end is painful and slow.

Real signal

If your team spends more than 50% of its time collecting and cleaning data rather than analysing it, you are at Level 1 — regardless of how sophisticated your tools look.

Level 2 — Compliance and control

Finance has established control frameworks, meets compliance requirements reliably, and produces consistent reporting. The team is respected but still primarily backward-looking. Budgets are controlled but forecasts are rarely trusted. Business partners consult finance to check costs, not to shape strategy.

Level 3 — Business partnership

Finance is embedded in business decisions. Finance business partners attend operational meetings, challenge assumptions, and help build financial models for initiatives. Dashboards are used by non-finance leaders to manage performance. Month-end has been shortened significantly through process improvement.

Level 4 — Strategic decision engine

Finance drives business strategy. The CFO operates as a genuine strategic co-pilot to the CEO. Finance produces scenario models before major decisions are made. Automation handles the transactional layer. The team's primary output is insight and foresight — not historical reports.

The diagnostic framework

To identify where your finance function sits, evaluate it across five dimensions:

  • Process efficiency: How much manual work exists in standard cycles?
  • Data quality and availability: How trusted and accessible is your financial data?
  • Reporting purpose: Are reports produced for compliance or for decisions?
  • Business partnership depth: How embedded is finance in operational and strategic conversations?
  • Technology leverage: Are you using your tools to their potential, or are they mostly spreadsheets?
From practice — Stellantis

When I joined the transformation programme at Stellantis, the finance function was solidly at Level 2 — compliant, controlled, but consuming enormous time on manual reporting. The shift to Level 3 came primarily from two interventions: automating the R2R cycle and rebuilding the dashboard architecture in Power BI. Within 6 months, weekly reporting that had required 3 days of manual work was being distributed automatically to 70+ leaders.

What this means for your transformation

The maturity model is not just a diagnostic — it is a prioritisation tool. Moving from Level 1 to Level 2 requires different interventions than moving from Level 2 to Level 3.

Organisations stuck at Level 1 need process standardisation and basic automation before they can think about dashboards or business partnering. Jumping straight to advanced analytics when the data is unreliable and the processes are manual is a guaranteed waste of time and money.

The rest of this module will give you the specific diagnostic tools to assess your current state accurately — and module 2 will address the highest-leverage intervention at each level.
