Finance Process Automation at Scale

How SAP S/4HANA integration, RPA deployment, and Power BI dashboards transformed finance operations across multiple European entities — eliminating manual workload and building executive-grade visibility.

Organisation Stellantis (Automotive)
Geography Multiple European entities
Focus areas R2R · P2P · OTC · Reporting
Technologies SAP S/4HANA · RPA · Power BI

The situation

When I joined the transformation programme, the finance function was producing accurate, compliant reports — but at an enormous cost in time and manual effort. Finance teams across multiple European entities were each running their own reporting processes, using different tools, different templates, and different definitions of the same metrics.

Month-end close was consuming 3–5 days of intensive manual work per entity. Reconciliations were done in spreadsheets. Data was extracted from SAP, processed in Excel, formatted manually, and emailed to leadership teams — a cycle that left almost no time for actual analysis.

The weekly management report — theoretically the most important piece of financial communication in the business — was assembled manually by two analysts, took two full days to produce, and was consistently late.

The core challenge

The challenge was not technical. It was structural and political. Each entity had its own way of doing things. Some teams were protective of their processes. Some leaders did not believe automation was relevant for finance. The SAP instance was used differently by different entities, which created data inconsistency at the source.

Key insight

The biggest risk in this type of transformation is automating the wrong thing — taking a broken process and making it faster. The first step was not deployment. It was diagnosis. Every process had to be understood, questioned, and redesigned before a single automation was built.

The approach

The intervention had three phases:

Phase 1 — Process mapping and redesign

Each major finance process across R2R, P2P, and OTC was mapped in detail — including every manual step, every system touchpoint, every handoff, and every decision point. The maps revealed significant variation between entities, multiple redundant steps, and several processes that could simply be eliminated.

Before any automation was designed, each process was redesigned. Steps were eliminated where possible. Definitions were standardised. Data fields in SAP were aligned across entities.

Phase 2 — Automation deployment

With clean, standardised processes in place, automation was deployed in layers:

  • RPA bots were deployed for the highest-volume data extraction and reconciliation tasks — particularly around intercompany matching and SAP data pulls
  • Power Automate flows were built for approval workflows, exception alerts, and automated data routing between systems
  • Python scripts handled more complex data transformation tasks that were outside the scope of low-code tools

Phase 3 — Dashboard and reporting transformation

The manual weekly report was completely rebuilt as a Power BI dashboard. A single source of truth was established in a data model that connected SAP extracts, processed data, and KPI calculations. The dashboard was published to a shared workspace and distributed automatically to a distribution list of 70+ leaders across Europe, on schedule, every week — without manual intervention.

What changed

The same weekly report that had taken two analysts two days to produce was now generated and distributed automatically, every Monday morning, at 7:30am — before most people had finished their first coffee. The analysts were no longer compiling. They were analysing.

The outcomes

The transformation delivered measurable improvements across every dimension that had been identified as problematic at the outset:

  • Weekly reporting cycle reduced from 2 days to fully automated
  • Month-end close time reduced significantly across entities
  • Manual data entry volume reduced by more than 60% in the highest-volume processes
  • Data consistency across entities improved, enabling cross-entity comparison for the first time
  • Finance team time freed for value-adding analysis rather than data production

What this taught me

Three lessons from this engagement that I now embed in every transformation project and training program:

1. Diagnose before you build

The temptation to start deploying automation immediately is strong — especially when the pain is obvious. But automating a broken process is one of the most expensive mistakes a transformation team can make. The diagnostic phase, even when it felt like it was slowing things down, was what made the outcomes possible.

2. Standardisation before automation

Automation amplifies what is already there. If the process is inconsistent across entities, automation will make it consistently inconsistent — and faster. Standardisation of definitions, templates, and SAP configuration was a prerequisite, not a nice-to-have.

3. Adoption requires leadership visibility

The weekly dashboard was adopted immediately and enthusiastically because leadership could see the value within the first week. Getting a quick win that is visible to senior stakeholders is not just good politics — it builds the trust and momentum that every transformation needs to sustain itself.
